Signs You Need a Vet Now

  • Difficulty breathing, open-mouth breathing, or panting (never normal in a cat)
  • Straining to urinate with little or no output, or crying in the litter box
  • A seizure lasting more than 2–3 minutes, or more than one in a day
  • Collapse, unresponsiveness, or inability to stand or walk normally
  • Uncontrolled bleeding, or pale, white, or blue-tinged gums
  • A hard, swollen, or painful abdomen
  • Known or suspected ingestion of something toxic
  • Any trauma — hit by a car, a fall from height, or an animal attack — even if the cat seems fine afterward

If You Suspect Poisoning

  • Do NOT induce vomiting unless a vet or poison control specifically tells you to
  • Bring the packaging, plant, or a sample of what was eaten — it changes what the vet can do
  • If the substance touched the skin or fur, rinse with water for about 15 minutes before you leave
  • Call your regular vet first if reachable; otherwise go straight to a 24hr clinic

Dangerous Foods

  • Onion & garlic (cooked, raw, or powdered)
  • Chocolate, especially dark
  • Grapes & raisins
  • Xylitol (a sugar substitute in sugar-free gum and baked goods)
  • Raw bread dough
  • Milk and dairy — not usually fatal, but most adult cats are lactose intolerant

Toxic Plants

  • Lilies — the most dangerous; even pollen on fur, licked off, can cause kidney failure
  • Sago palm — every part is toxic, can cause liver failure
  • Aloe vera, poinsettia, dracaena, cyclamen, asparagus fern, jade, snake plant

CPR

This guide doesn't yet cover feline CPR technique, which needs to come from a vet-reviewed source rather than be written from memory — compression rate and depth for a cat differ meaningfully from human or dog CPR, and getting it wrong here would do more harm than leaving it blank. If your cat is unresponsive and not breathing, call one of the numbers above immediately — some clinics can talk you through it live.
